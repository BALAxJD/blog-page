Chandrayaan-4 vs. Chandrayaan-3: How India’s Next Moon Mission Is going to More Complex, Bold, and Groundbreaking

India’s successful Chandrayaan-3 mission paved the way for advanced lunar exploration. Now, with Chandrayaan-4, ISRO is planning to take the next giant leap by attempting a lunar sample return mission—one of the most challenging feats in space exploration.

From Soft Landing to Sample Return

While Chandrayaan-3 focused on demonstrating India’s ability to perform a soft landing on the Moon and deploy a rover, Chandrayaan-4 sets a bolder target—bringing back lunar samples to Earth. This two-way mission includes an orbiter, lander, rover, and a mini ascent vehicle to return with Moon soil and rock samples.

Upgraded Technology and Precision

Chandrayaan-4 will use enhanced AI-based navigation systems to improve landing precision, and more advanced tools for drilling and collecting samples. Its instruments will be capable of storing regolith under cryogenic conditions to preserve its original properties during re-entry.

Strategic Scientific Objectives

Apart from proving technological capabilities, Chandrayaan-4 aims to investigate the Moon’s geology, especially near the south pole where water ice is believed to exist. By studying these samples, ISRO hopes to contribute critical knowledge to global lunar science and future manned missions.

India’s Leap Toward Deeper Space Exploration

Chandrayaan-4 marks a shift in ISRO’s goals—from demonstration missions to scientific return missions. It positions India alongside global space giants like NASA, Roscosmos, and CNSA. The mission could open doors to Mars exploration and crewed Moon missions in the future.

Conclusion

The leap from Chandrayaan-3 to Chandrayaan-4 isn’t just about engineering—it reflects India’s growing confidence in space. Chandrayaan-4 is not only technically ambitious but also symbolically powerful, showcasing India’s readiness to contribute meaningfully to humanity’s lunar future.
