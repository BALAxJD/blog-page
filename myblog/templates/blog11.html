Chandrayaan-4: ISRO's Mission to Return Moon Samples by 2028, Advancing India's Space Exploration Goals

India's space agency ISRO has announced its most ambitious lunar mission yet - Chandrayaan-4, aiming to bring Moon samples to Earth by 2028. This groundbreaking endeavor comes just months after the historic success of Chandrayaan-3's lunar landing.

Mission Architecture and Objectives

Chandrayaan-4 represents a significant technological leap from its predecessors, featuring a complex three-module design: an orbiter (2,800 kg), lander-rover combo (1,200 kg), and a revolutionary ascent vehicle (500 kg) that will launch the lunar samples back to Earth. The mission specifically targets the Moon's south pole to collect 500 grams of regolith from permanently shadowed regions, which may contain water ice and rare elements like Helium-3.

Technical Innovations

ISRO is developing cutting-edge systems for this mission, including an AI-powered autonomous landing system capable of precision touchdown within 100 meters of target sites in the rugged polar terrain. The rover will carry a sophisticated drilling mechanism to collect samples from up to 1 meter below the surface, stored in cryogenic containers at -150°C to prevent contamination during the return journey. The ascent vehicle must achieve lunar orbit rendezvous with the orbiter module - a maneuver never before attempted by ISRO.

Budget and Timeline

With an approved budget of ₹1,200 crore (~$150 million), the mission exemplifies ISRO's signature cost-effective approach. The development timeline includes critical design reviews in 2025, integrated system tests in 2026, and launch vehicle upgrades in 2027, targeting a Q2 2028 launch window. This schedule positions India to potentially become the fourth nation to return lunar samples after the US, Russia, and China.

Global Context and Challenges

Chandrayaan-4 enters a new era of lunar exploration alongside NASA's Artemis program and China's planned 2026 sample return. ISRO faces significant challenges in mastering Earth re-entry at hypervelocity (11 km/sec) and ensuring sample integrity during the violent descent. As ISRO Chairman S. Somanath noted: "This mission will demonstrate technologies critical for our future Gaganyaan manned space program." Success could pave the way for India's proposed crewed lunar mission in the 2030s.
