Top 10 Books to Read Before They're Adapted to Movies

With so many popular books being adapted into movies every year, it’s important to read these literary works before the filmmakers put their own spin on them. Here are the top 10 books that should be read before their movie adaptations hit the screens.

The Night Circus by Erin Morgenstern

A magical, mysterious, and visually captivating tale set in a circus that only appears at night. As two young illusionists are forced into a competition, the stakes are higher than they could have imagined. Morgenstern's lyrical prose and the circus's enchanting atmosphere will captivate readers. The movie adaptation promises to be as visually stunning as the book, so it's a must-read before it hits the theaters.

Ready Player One by Ernest Cline

Set in a dystopian future where most people escape into a virtual reality world, "Ready Player One" is a thrilling adventure. The book’s fast-paced action, pop-culture references, and deep themes make it a standout. The movie adaptation, directed by Steven Spielberg, has already sparked excitement, but the book offers a rich, detailed world that can't be fully captured on screen.

The Goldfinch by Donna Tartt

Winner of the Pulitzer Prize, "The Goldfinch" is an intricate, emotional journey about loss, art, and survival. With its richly layered narrative and complex characters, it’s a challenging read, but its depth is what makes it so powerful. The movie adaptation is coming soon, so it’s the perfect time to experience this literary masterpiece before it’s condensed into a film.

Where the Crawdads Sing by Delia Owens

This best-selling novel is set in the marshes of North Carolina and follows the life of Kya Clark, a young woman who grows up isolated from society. The haunting atmosphere, combined with themes of abandonment, nature, and survival, makes this book a gripping read. The movie adaptation is highly anticipated, so be sure to read the book first to fully appreciate its beauty.

Little Women by Louisa May Alcott

A classic that continues to resonate with readers of all ages, "Little Women" follows the lives of the four March sisters as they navigate family, love, and loss. With the recent movie adaptation directed by Greta Gerwig, now is the perfect time to revisit this heartwarming story that has stood the test of time.

Big Little Lies by Liane Moriarty

This psychological drama explores the lives of three women in an affluent neighborhood, all of whom are hiding dark secrets. Moriarty’s twisty, suspenseful narrative makes for an addictive read. The HBO series adaptation has already made waves, but the book offers more depth and character development, making it an essential read.

The Silent Patient by Alex Michaelides

A psychological thriller that will keep you on the edge of your seat, "The Silent Patient" follows the story of Alicia Berenson, a celebrated artist who shoots her husband and then goes mute. The twisty narrative and unpredictable ending make this a must-read before the movie adaptation hits theaters.

Circe by Madeline Miller

A retelling of the myth of Circe, the Greek goddess, this novel combines classical mythology with rich, modern storytelling. Miller’s lyrical prose and exploration of power, identity, and transformation make this a standout book. The movie adaptation promises to be stunning, but reading the book offers a deeper connection to the characters and themes.

The Martian by Andy Weir

A gripping survival story set on Mars, "The Martian" follows astronaut Mark Watney as he struggles to stay alive after being stranded on the Red Planet. The movie adaptation, starring Matt Damon, was already a hit, but the book offers a much deeper exploration of Watney’s technical challenges and humor.

All the Light We Cannot See by Anthony Doerr

Set during World War II, this novel follows a blind French girl and a German soldier whose lives intersect in occupied France. The book’s beauty lies in its lyrical prose and the way it captures the human cost of war. The movie adaptation promises to be poignant, but the book’s lyrical style adds layers of meaning that may be lost on screen.
